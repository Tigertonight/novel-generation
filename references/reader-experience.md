# Reader Experience Protocol

Use this protocol to prevent long-novel generation from becoming structurally correct but emotionally flat. It is especially important in the opening movement, where readers decide whether they care. Do not hard-code this as a fixed chapter count; calibrate by story length, genre, and serialization rhythm.

## Opening Movement

Opening Movement means the early portion before the story settles into its long middle.

Calibrate by format:
- short novella: first scene or first chapter
- compact novel: the first sustained sequence that establishes the central pressure
- long web novel: first arc or first major sequence
- multi-volume work: first volume's opening arc

The opening movement ends when the old situation is no longer recoverable and the main dramatic engine is clearly running.

## Opening Reader Promises

Early in the opening movement, the reader should know:
- who the protagonist appears to be
- what pressure or wound makes the protagonist interesting
- what kind of world this story takes place in
- what public/private stakes are already in motion
- why the premise cannot be solved by a simple explanation

By the end of the opening movement, the reader should have:
- several memorable character impressions
- several naturally dramatized world/background facts
- more than one stage conflict or reversal
- one mini-climax that changes the reader's understanding of the premise

## Character Impression Anchors

For every major character introduced in the opening movement, design a compact entrance card before drafting:

```markdown
## Character Entrance Card
- Name / role:
- Exterior signal: one visible detail, silhouette, object, clothing, body language, or recurring gesture
- Behavioral signature: how they act under pressure
- Contradiction: what makes them not one-note
- First scene pressure: what forces them to reveal character
- Reader memory hook: what the reader should remember after the scene
```

Use physical description sparingly but specifically. Avoid generic labels such as "calm", "cold", "beautiful", or "smart" without a visible behavior that proves it.

Good character entrance patterns:
- show the character making a costly choice
- show the character lying to someone who trusts them
- show the character refusing an easy solution
- show how others react to the character
- give the character a pressure habit or object that can recur

## Worldbuilding Through Action

Do not front-load encyclopedic background. Reveal world/background through:
- public consequences: trending topics, legal notices, family pressure, contracts, rankings, sanctions
- institutional procedures: company incident response, league rules, platform moderation, sponsor pressure
- material spaces: office layout, arena backstage, old internet cafe, team dorm, streamer room
- economic incentives: who gets paid, who loses contracts, who can be sacrificed
- constraints: what characters cannot say, cannot access, cannot prove, or cannot safely reveal

Across the opening movement, every chapter or major scene should expose at least one world/context fact through a scene-level action or cost.

## Pacing Curve And Stage Conflict

A chapter should not only deliver information. It should turn.

Useful chapter turns:
- discovery becomes personal cost
- ally becomes suspect
- evidence creates a worse dilemma
- a plan works but harms someone
- a character wins access by losing moral ground
- public pressure forces a private lie
- a safe explanation becomes impossible

For the opening movement, plan a visible mini-arc. The exact chapter numbers may change, but the rhythm should exist:

```text
Opening hook / abnormal event
Personal cost of entering the premise
Second viewpoint or relationship complicates truth
Opposing force shows worldview
First hidden system/world rule appears
Mirror test or early confrontation
Public/emotional cost lands
Old wound or world history surfaces
Protagonist makes morally costly choice
Mini-climax/reversal changes the premise
```

This is a default rhythm, not a formula. Adjust by genre, but keep the principle: early chapters need rising pressure, not only setup.

## Early Reader-Experience Audit

At natural checkpoints in the opening movement, run a short reader-facing audit. Typical checkpoints are after the opening hook, after the second viewpoint/relationship complication, after the first major confrontation, and after the opening mini-climax:

```markdown
## Early Reader Experience Audit
- Main character impression: clear / blurry
- Physical or behavioral anchors present:
- World/background visible through action:
- Strongest emotional question:
- Strongest conflict or reversal:
- Flat/procedural sections:
- Repair recommendation:
```

If two consecutive early chapters are mostly explanation, investigation, or clue transfer, repair before continuing. Add a scene-level choice, cost, relationship fracture, public consequence, or reversal.

## Repair Moves

If characters feel blurry:
- add a first-impression action, not a biography
- add one recurring object/gesture
- put the character under pressure earlier
- make another character misread them or challenge them

If worldbuilding feels invisible:
- make an institution act on the character
- show a contract, rule, ranking, public scandal, or physical workplace
- make a background fact cost someone money, status, access, or safety

If pacing feels flat:
- add a false choice
- make the clue create danger instead of clarity
- make the protagonist's correct decision hurt someone
- end the chapter with a changed relationship, not just new information
