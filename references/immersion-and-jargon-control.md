# Immersion And Jargon Control

Use this reference when chapters feel too external, camera-only, report-like, industry-heavy, procedural, or full of professional terminology that interrupts the reader's emotional experience.

The default goal for commercial fiction is not to prove expertise. The goal is to make readers care about what the expertise costs, reveals, threatens, or changes between people.

## Narrative Distance Contract

Before drafting, define the target distance in `bible/style-bible.md`:

```text
POV mode: first person / close third / medium third / omniscient / rotating close third
Default distance: close / medium-close / cinematic with interior beats / cool observer
Interior density: low / medium / high
Inner monologue style: direct thought / free indirect style / bodily reaction / memory flash / suppressed thought
Exterior-interior ratio:
When to zoom in:
When to pull back:
Forbidden habit: camera-only summary, report voice, detached event listing
```

For most relationship-driven, webnovel, romance, revenge, career-growth, and character-led projects, prefer close or medium-close narration with frequent interior reaction beats.

## Interior Beat Types

Use varied interiority instead of only "he thought/she thought":

- Instinctive reaction: the first unfiltered response before the character controls it.
- Suppressed thought: what the character refuses to say or even finish thinking.
- Body-mind bridge: breath, heat, nausea, numbness, pulse, muscle tension, delayed pain.
- Memory shard: a short remembered image or phrase that changes the present.
- Misread/interpretation: how the POV explains another person's action, correctly or incorrectly.
- Desire/fear flash: what the POV wants, and why wanting it is dangerous.
- Self-deception: the lie the POV tells themselves in the moment.
- Emotional aftertaste: what remains after a conversation, touch, win, loss, or humiliation.
- Moral recoil: the private cost of a choice that looks correct from outside.
- Relational calculation: what this moment means for trust, status, attraction, debt, or resentment.

## Scene Immersion Packet

Add this to chapter packets when immersion is at risk:

```text

Subtext they miss:
Memory/wound touched:

```

Every important scene should have an interior turn, not only an external result.

## Close-Third Writing Rules

- Filter the scene through what the POV values, fears, desires, misunderstands, and lacks language for.
- Use free indirect style when useful: the narration can briefly sound like the character's private logic without quotation marks.
- Let external details be selected by emotion. A jealous POV notices different things than a frightened or ambitious POV.
- After major dialogue, action, intimacy, humiliation, or victory, land a brief inner aftershock.
- Avoid explaining the character's entire psychology. Give enough interior pressure for the reader to feel the choice.
- Do not keep the camera outside for many paragraphs during emotionally important scenes.

## Professional / Industry Content Rule

Professional content must be translated into story pressure.

Before including a technical term, industry process, workplace detail, law, finance, entertainment, medicine, gaming, business, military, or academic concept, ask:

```text
Does the reader need the term itself, or only the consequence?
Whose desire or fear does this term affect?
What relationship changes because of it?
What choice, leverage, humiliation, risk, money, status, access, or intimacy does it create?
Can it be shown through action, a document, a deadline, a public reaction, or a concrete cost?
Can the explanation be delayed until the reader cares?
```

If the term does not create pressure, cut it or replace it with a plain consequence.

## Jargon Translation Ladder

Use the lowest rung that preserves the story:

```text
1. Consequence only: "If this failed, she would lose the contract by morning."
2. Plain-language term: "the sponsor could walk away"
3. One precise term with immediate context
4. Short explanation through conflict or dialogue
5. Full professional explanation only when the scene's pleasure is competence, deduction, or strategy
```

Default to rungs 1-3. Use rung 5 sparingly and only when expertise is itself the payoff.

## Career / Workline Compression

For career, workplace, business, officialdom, entertainment, legal, gaming, or other professional arcs:

```text
Career beat purpose:
Emotional stake:
Relationship stake:
Public/status stake:
Concrete decision:
Professional detail needed:
Professional detail to omit:
Plain consequence:
Scene exit change:
```

Rules:
- Work scenes should usually be shorter than relationship, conflict, payoff, or emotional consequence scenes unless the genre promise is workplace procedural.
- Do not make several consecutive scenes primarily about process, meetings, documents, metrics, strategy, or terminology.
- If a professional scene is necessary, attach it to rivalry, attraction, betrayal, public pressure, humiliation, moral choice, or a visible reward.
- Expertise should appear as behavior under pressure, not as a lecture.
- Let readers feel "what this means" before asking them to understand "how this works."

## Exposition Budget

For each chapter, set a rough budget:

```text
Max new proper nouns:
Max new technical terms:
Max institutional/process explanation paragraphs:
Required plain-language consequence for each technical detail:
Emotional beat required after exposition:
```

Suggested default for webnovel/commercial chapters:
- 0-2 new technical terms.
- No more than one compact process explanation unless the chapter is a strategy set piece.
- Every technical term must be followed by a consequence, reaction, or changed relationship.

## Audit Questions

```text
Did the chapter spend enough time inside the POV character?
Are there interior beats after major external beats?
Does each important scene have an interior turn?
Could the same scene be filmed silently with no loss? If yes, add inner pressure.
Are professional terms necessary, or can they become plain consequences?
Did any terminology interrupt emotion, tension, or relationship movement?
Are career/work scenes serving story, character, relationship, or payoff?
Did the chapter privilege process over people?
```

P0:
- POV promise is broken by detached report narration across a major emotional chapter.
- Professional exposition blocks the required reader promise or makes the chapter mostly process with no story movement.

P1:
- Important scene lacks interior reaction or aftermath.
- Too many professional terms appear without plain consequences.
- Work/career scene moves facts but not character, relationship, status, or pressure.
- Dialogue explains process when action, cost, or conflict could show it.
