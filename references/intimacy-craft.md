# Intimacy Craft

Use this reference when romance, desire, sexual tension, marriage, harem/multi-heroine dynamics, or intimate scenes are part of the reader contract.

Intimacy is not only "sex scene insertion." It is a relationship engine: attention, restraint, fantasy, jealousy, vulnerability, power exchange, trust, risk, aftercare, regret, tenderness, leverage, and changed behavior. Plan it before drafting, then write it according to the project's explicitness boundary.

## Boundary First

Before designing intimate scenes, define:

```text
Age/status boundary: all intimate parties must be adult and capable of consent.
Consent model: enthusiastic / negotiated / complicated-but-consensual / fade-to-black only.
Explicitness level: none / subtext / sensual / fade-to-black / explicit adult scene.
Taboos to avoid:
Power imbalance handling:
POV comfort: whose desire, shame, restraint, or control is being followed.
Reader promise: romantic tension / erotic charge / tenderness / dangerous attraction / healing / possession / mutual conquest / other.
```

Do not write sexualized minors, coercive sex as titillation, assault as reward, or intimate scenes that erase agency. If a dark or coercive event is part of the plot, treat it as harm and consequence, not erotic payoff.

Create this for each important romantic/intimate pair or triangle:


## Heat Ladder

Use the ladder to pace attraction. Do not skip from zero to explicit intimacy unless the genre promise, character psychology, and relationship state support it.

```text
0. Awareness: notices body, voice, scent, danger, competence, status, or vulnerability
1. Friction: banter, rivalry, irritation, misread motive, charged refusal
2. Attention: remembers small details, tracks mood, notices absence
3. Proximity: forced closeness, shared space, secrecy, travel, danger, care
4. Protective impulse: rescue, cover, lie, public defense, jealousy, possessiveness
5. Touch threshold: almost-touch, accidental touch, healing, training, clothing, wound care
6. Private vulnerability: confession, fear, shame, old wound, weakness seen
7. Desire named indirectly: jokes, restraint, avoidance, dream, jealousy, confession through action
8. Boundary negotiation: what is allowed, refused, delayed, risked
9. Intimacy: kiss, fade-to-black, sensual scene, explicit adult scene according to boundary
10. Aftermath: tenderness, awkwardness, changed power, secret, guilt, commitment, rupture, danger
```

Good heat scenes usually move at least two tracks: physical proximity plus emotional cost, desire plus restraint, care plus suspicion, vulnerability plus leverage, intimacy plus future danger.

## Scene Function Types

Choose the function before writing:

- Temptation scene: desire threatens a goal, vow, disguise, alliance, or moral line.
- Care scene: wound care, fatigue, illness, grooming, clothing, food, shelter, or protection creates intimacy.
- Power scene: one character has leverage; the scene tests whether they exploit it.
- Jealousy scene: desire becomes visible through reaction, not declaration.
- Boundary scene: someone says yes, no, not yet, never again, or only under changed terms.
- Trust scene: a private vulnerability is shown and not used as a weapon.
- Rupture scene: intimacy makes the relationship worse because fear, secrecy, guilt, or outside pressure enters.
- Repair scene: apology, changed behavior, sacrifice, restraint, or accountability rebuilds heat.
- Consummation scene: physical intimacy changes relationship state, stakes, secrets, status, or future choices.
- Aftermath scene: the real meaning appears after the high point.

## Writing Mechanism

For each intimate or charged scene, assemble:

```text
Scene function:
Starting relationship state:
Ending relationship state:
POV desire:
POV fear/restraint:
Other person's readable desire:
Boundary/consent beat:
Physical choreography:
Sensory palette:
Subtext:
Interruption/risk:
Emotional turn:
Aftermath hook:
Ledger updates:
```

Drafting rules:
- Write desire through selection: what the POV notices and what they avoid noticing.
- Use body language as action, not decoration: distance, breath, hands, posture, clothes, wounds, voice, stillness.
- Keep consent and agency visible at the chosen explicitness level.
- Make the scene irreversible in some way: new trust, new secret, changed leverage, public risk, emotional dependency, fear, guilt, commitment, or rupture.
- Avoid generic heat words. Use character-specific desire: what this person finds dangerous, beautiful, forbidden, comforting, or humiliating.
- Do not make every intimate scene a reward after victory. Sometimes heat is temptation, cost, misstep, negotiation, or vulnerability.

## Explicitness Levels

```text
Subtext:
  attraction carried by attention, refusal, silence, jealousy, care, and misread motives.

Sensual:
  touch, proximity, breath, clothing, warmth, restraint, bodily awareness, but no graphic sexual detail.

Fade-to-black:
  scene escalates to a clear threshold, then cuts to aftermath where emotional and plot consequences land.

Explicit adult scene:
  only when the user wants it, all parties are adult and consenting, and the scene has a narrative function beyond arousal.
```

Even explicit scenes still need story movement: relationship redefinition, leverage change, secret exposure, vow, guilt, risk, or future consequence.

## Aftermath Is Mandatory

After major intimacy, update:

```text
Relationship trust:
Attachment or dependency:
Power balance:
Boundary changed:
Secret created or exposed:
Public risk:
Jealousy/rival reaction:
Character self-image:
Next behavior change:
Next test:
```

If nothing changes afterward, the scene is detachable and should be repaired or cut.

## Audit Questions

```text
Did the scene match the declared explicitness boundary?
Were all parties adult and capable of consent?
Was desire character-specific rather than generic?
Did the scene include agency, restraint, or boundary?
Did the relationship state change?
Did the physical choreography make sense?
Did the scene affect plot, leverage, trust, secrecy, status, or future choices?
Was there an aftermath beat or planned aftermath?
Did this intimacy beat vary from previous beats?
```

P0:
- Consent, age, or taboo boundary is violated.
- Intimacy contradicts established relationship state without cause.
- A required relationship/romance promise is skipped for too long in a romance-forward project.

P1:
- The scene is generic and could belong to any pair.
- The scene has no aftermath or relationship-state change.
- Heat jumps too far without setup.
- Physical detail exists but emotional stakes are missing.
