# Screenwriting Craft Integration

This reference adapts useful screenwriting principles for long-form prose. Use it as a craft layer on top of the novel production system: the novel workflow owns continuity and long-task control; this layer improves character depth, scene tension, pacing, and reader engagement.

The source inspiration is screenwriting methodology such as dramatic action, character arc design, value shifts, dual-track pacing, scene breakdown, and script-doctor review. Do not force screenplay formatting into prose. Borrow the thinking, not the output format.

## 1. Cross-Section Principle

A story should feel like it begins inside a world that has already been running.

Before drafting the opening movement, know:
- what happened before chapter 1
- what social, economic, emotional, or institutional pressure already exists
- what relationships are already damaged, indebted, affectionate, or unequal
- why this exact opening moment is the best cut into the character's life

Do not explain all of this immediately. Use it to make the first scenes feel lived-in.

## 2. Dramatic Action

Every major scene should have:

```text
Goal -> Obstacle -> Result
```

Goal:
: What does a character actively want in this scene?

Obstacle:
: Who or what resists them?

Result:
: What changes because of the attempt? Success, failure, partial success, or unexpected turn are all valid.

If a scene only transfers information, repair it. Add pursuit, resistance, cost, or a changed relationship.

## 3. Character Depth Chain

For major characters, build this chain in `bible/character-bible.md`:

```markdown
## Character Craft Chain
- Ghost: the prior wound, debt, shame, loss, or formative event
- Lie: the false belief formed from the Ghost
- Flaw: the behavior pattern caused by the Lie
- Want: the external goal the character knowingly pursues
- Need: the truth or capacity the character avoids but must face
- Truth: the hard-earned counter-belief
- Arc: beginning state -> false change/regression -> crisis choice -> irreversible change/new normal
```

Use the chain to generate action, not exposition. The reader should infer Ghost/Lie/Flaw through behavior, avoidance, recurring gestures, choices, and relationships before it is named.

## 4. Arc Budget

Long fiction should not let characters fully transform too early. Treat character change as a limited budget distributed across the whole story.

Plan:
- early movement: Lie and Flaw are established or reinforced
- early middle: character gets partial wins using old methods
- midpoint: false victory or false defeat changes the game
- late middle: Flaw creates real cost; a false change collapses
- crisis: character faces a real dilemma where both choices cost something
- climax: internal change becomes external action
- ending: show the new normal through behavior, not explanation

For serialized or very long novels, use arc budgets by volume or season-like arc.

## 5. Value Shift

Every chapter and major scene should change a meaningful value.

Examples:
- trust -> suspicion
- safety -> danger
- control -> exposure
- clean -> compromised
- freedom -> obligation
- hope -> dread
- isolation -> alliance
- ignorance -> burdened knowledge

Ask:
- What value is active at the start?
- What value is active at the end?
- Did the reader feel a shift, or did the chapter merely add facts?

If no value changes, the scene may be filler or should be merged.

## 6. Dual-Track Pacing

Track two lines separately:

External plot intensity:
: event density, danger, confrontation, reveals, public consequences.

Internal emotional intensity:
: shame, grief, dread, desire, trust, betrayal, intimacy, moral pressure.

Useful combinations:
- external high + internal high: climax, exposure, confrontation
- external low + internal high: quiet scene with heavy emotional charge
- external high + internal low: tense action with irony, competence, or dark humor
- external low + internal low: breath, reset, texture; use sparingly

Avoid long stretches where both tracks stay flat. After intense chapters, plan breath; after breath, plan pressure.

## 7. World Reveal Layers

For complex settings, plan three layers:

```markdown
## World Reveal Layers
- Surface rules: what everyone thinks the world is
- Operating logic: how power, money, technology, magic, law, or culture actually works
- Bottom truth: what redefines the reader's understanding of earlier events
```

Opening movement should make the surface rules visible through action and cost. The middle should reveal operating logic. The late story should expose the bottom truth.

Worldbuilding should arrive through:
- what characters can and cannot do
- institutions acting on people
- public reaction and consequences
- contracts, rules, rankings, rituals, interfaces, architecture
- money, status, access, danger, or social shame

## 8. Subtext And Action

Prose may use interiority, but do not lean on explanation when behavior would carry more force.

Prefer:
- a character avoiding a question
- a repeated gesture changing meaning
- a polite conversation with hostile stakes
- an object treated differently after a reveal
- someone doing the opposite of what they claim

Dialogue should often solve the surface problem while revealing a deeper conflict indirectly.

## 9. Secondary Character Function

Important secondary characters should not exist only to deliver information.

Assign at least one function:
- mirror: shows what the protagonist could become
- catalyst: forces change or action
- thematic counterpoint: embodies another answer to the story question
- pressure source: raises stakes and constraints
- relief/breath: changes rhythm without becoming irrelevant

Give recurring secondary characters:
- their own Want
- one contradiction
- one relationship lever with the protagonist
- a micro-arc or state change if they stay in the story

## 10. Opening Hook And Movement

Opening Movement means the early portion before the story settles into its long middle. It is not a fixed chapter count.

Calibrate by format:
- short novella: first scene or first chapter
- compact novel: the first sustained sequence that establishes the central pressure
- long web novel: first arc or first major sequence
- multi-volume work: first volume's opening arc

The opening movement should:
- show the protagonist under pressure
- reveal enough world/context that the premise feels grounded
- make at least one relationship or public consequence visible
- produce a concrete dramatic question
- end with a turn that makes the old situation impossible

## 11. Craft Audit

Use this during chapter, batch, and final audits:

```markdown
## Screenwriting Craft Audit
- Dramatic action: Does each major scene have goal -> obstacle -> result?
- Value shift: What changed from start to end?
- Character chain: Which Ghost/Lie/Flaw/Want/Need pressure is active?
- Arc budget: Did the character change too much, too little, or regress usefully?
- Dual-track pacing: External intensity / internal intensity / needed contrast?
- World reveal: Surface rule, operating logic, or bottom truth revealed naturally?
- Subtext: Any dialogue explaining what action could show?
- Secondary roles: Are recurring side characters functional and alive?
- Repair: Add choice, cost, resistance, reversal, or behavior-based subtext.
```
