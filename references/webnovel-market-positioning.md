# Webnovel Market Positioning

Use this reference before architecture when designing commercial webnovels, serialized genre fiction, male-channel/female-channel projects, platform-facing stories, or any project where topic selection and reader contract matter.

This is the baseline industry-knowledge layer. It works even without browsing. When current market taste matters, combine it with `webnovel-market-research.md`, which gathers fresh public signals from platforms, rankings, book pages, and open reviews.

Do not treat male-channel/female-channel as biological rules or rigid formulas. Treat them as common market lanes and reader-contract patterns. A project may be male-channel, female-channel, cross-channel, genre-first, literary, experimental, or deliberately anti-type.

## Male-Channel Reader Contracts

Common male-channel projects often center agency expansion: the protagonist moves from blocked, underestimated, ordinary, low-status, betrayed, poor, weak, unknown, or constrained into increasing power, status, freedom, territory, knowledge, wealth, mastery, recognition, or order-building.

Common pleasures:
- visible reversal from low position
- fast proof of protagonist competence or hidden value
- upgrade, resource, rank, status, revenge, face-slap, secret, territory, or freedom payoffs
- public recognition after being underestimated
- enemy ladder from local pressure to larger system pressure
- protagonist team, faction, company, sect, kingdom, family, or territory expansion
- long climb toward "登顶", "翻身", "破局", "立规矩", or "掌控命运"

Common anxieties:
- protagonist is too passive
- wins feel unearned or too easy
- payoff comes too late
- cheat/system is unclear or limitless
- enemies are stupid only so the protagonist can win
- side characters only praise the protagonist
- multi-heroine design reduces women to rewards

Common story engines:
- underdog comeback / 咸鱼翻身
- rebirth with information advantage
- transmigration with modern knowledge or role mismatch
- system, panel, profession, game, cultivation, skill tree, or simulation
- business, farming, territory, sect, company, kingdom, officialdom, or family-building
- revenge, investigation, survival, military, apocalypse, urban power, or hidden identity
- "扮猪吃虎", public face-slap, enemy ladder, resource competition, rule-breaking competence

## Male-Channel Character Design

Protagonist design:
- starting disadvantage: status, money, power, body, reputation, access, family, knowledge, or social legitimacy
- hidden advantage: memory, system, craft, moral line, professional skill, future knowledge, unusual patience, or forbidden method
- active method: how he solves problems repeatedly but not effortlessly
- long flaw: arrogance, distrust, obsession, avoidance, recklessness, softness, revenge tunnel vision, or emotional illiteracy
- escalation vector: personal survival -> local recognition -> faction pressure -> institutional pressure -> world-order pressure

Supporting role modules:
- protagonist team: each member must have function, desire, limit, loyalty tension, and growth
- brother/comrade: emotional trust and action competence, not only cheerleading
- mentor/patron: access and worldview pressure, not only gifts
- rival: alternative version of the protagonist, not only a stepping stone
- antagonist ladder: local bully, professional rival, faction agent, system guardian, hidden hand, ideological opponent
- multi-heroine: each heroine needs agency, desire, boundary, personal arc, and consequence; avoid treating relationships as trophies
- single heroine/no heroine/relationship-light: make the choice explicit in reader contract and avoid accidental bait

## Female-Channel Reader Contracts

Common female-channel projects often center identity, emotional agency, social field, relationship pressure, self-determination, dignity recovery, and being seen correctly. Romance may be central, secondary, or absent; the deeper pattern is often "I regain the right to define my life."

Common pleasures:
- identity reversal, status correction, rebirth, transmigration, marriage/contract premise, career turn, family reversal, or public reputation repair
- emotional tension, restraint, intimacy, misunderstanding, rupture, repair, and changed choices
- protagonist competence under social, family, workplace, court, entertainment, school, clan, or marriage pressure
- satisfying correction of people who misread, exploit, replace, shame, or constrain the protagonist
- relationship state movement: distrust -> bargain -> alliance -> intimacy -> test -> repair or rupture
- ensemble emotional network: family, rivals, allies, friends, colleagues, children, elders, fans, or social witnesses

Common anxieties:
- protagonist's growth is solved only by romance
- love interest has power but no accountability
- rival women are flattened into jealousy machines
- family/workplace/court pressure is only melodrama, not a system
- emotional turns arrive without earned behavioral change
- suffering is repeated without new choice, leverage, or dignity

Common story engines:
- rebirth, transmigration, replacement, true/false daughter, fake marriage, contract relationship, divorce/remarriage, second chance
- entertainment industry, workplace, court, palace, wealthy family, school, era fiction, farming, medicine, law, business, or family management
- "先婚后爱", "追妻", "破镜", "身份错位", "逆袭打脸", "事业成长", "治愈救赎", "群像关系网"

## Female-Channel Character Design

Protagonist design:
- starting constraint: family, marriage, reputation, class, career, body, gendered expectation, secret, debt, trauma, or social role
- active choice: what she refuses, protects, pursues, exits, builds, or exposes
- dignity line: what she will not trade away for love, safety, status, or revenge
- competence field: career, social intelligence, craft, law, medicine, business, art, management, emotional clarity, survival, or strategy
- emotional arc: from misread/constrained to self-authored, not simply "chosen by the right person"

Relationship role modules:
- love interest: desire, fear, agency, accountability, and change; not only protection or wealth
- rival: competing desire or worldview, not only jealousy
- family member: debt, history, leverage, guilt, duty, or love with cost
- ally/friend: independent life and pressure, not only emotional support
- child/elder/mentor: ethical pressure and relational consequence
- workplace/court/social field: institutions must exert pressure through rules, reputation, money, access, law, etiquette, taboo, or public opinion

## Cross-Channel And Genre-First Projects

Use cross-channel positioning when the project blends:
- strong external plot with emotional/relationship depth
- action/progression with a clear relationship or group-bond engine
- mystery/investigation with romance, family, or social-field pressure
- career/industry/officialdom with intimate character arcs
- literary texture with serial hooks

For cross-channel work, state which promise leads and which promise supports. Do not try to maximize every module. Too many promises blur the first 3 chapters.

## Trope And Payoff Libraries

Use tropes as engines, not decorations.

Underdog / comeback:
- show the initial cage
- give the protagonist one concrete active method
- create a visible reversal
- leave a cost or new enemy

Face-slap / public correction:
- establish a false public belief
- give the opponent agency and leverage
- let the protagonist win through prepared action or competence
- include witnesses and consequence
- avoid empty humiliation with no plot movement

Upgrade / growth:
- define trigger, resource, limit, cost, failure mode, and next threshold
- make upgrades solve one problem while creating another

Rebirth / transmigration:
- define information advantage and blind spots
- prevent perfect prediction through changed timeline, emotional bias, or hidden variables
- let old wounds shape new choices

System / mechanism:
- define rule, reward, cost, loophole, failure mode, and enemy counter
- do not let the system replace character agency

Relationship pull:
- define what each side wants, fears, hides, and misunderstands
- move relationship state through action, not confession alone

Protagonist team:
- define each member's role, desire, limit, conflict with protagonist, and payoff path
- rotate who solves, who fails, who pays, and who changes

Long-serial expansion:
- expand map, stakes, faction pressure, resource scale, secret depth, emotional obligation, and cost
- avoid only adding stronger enemies without changing the story world

## First Three Chapters

The first three chapters should prove the reader contract.

Chapter 1:
- protagonist impression
- starting pressure
- hook or disruption
- first glimpse of trope engine
- one concrete choice or reaction

Chapter 2:
- consequence of the hook
- clearer mechanism or relationship pressure
- first active move
- first sign of opposition

Chapter 3:
- first payoff, reversal, emotional turn, or public consequence
- long promise becomes visible
- reason to keep reading beyond curiosity

Adjust by platform and genre. Some slow-burn female-channel, literary, or suspense projects may delay external payoff, but they still need emotional, relational, atmospheric, or mystery pressure early.

## Benchmark And Best-Practice Use

When using industry benchmarks:
- extract premise engine, hook timing, payoff rhythm, relationship structure, protagonist pressure, chapter-ending habit, and long-serial expansion method
- do not imitate living authors' distinctive prose
- do not copy character combinations, named settings, scene order, twists, or signature lines
- make an original positioning decision: "same reader pleasure, different mechanism/world/relationship/cost"

## Quality Check

Before architecture, ask:
- Can the hook be understood in one sentence?
- Does the first 3 chapters prove the promise?
- Is the protagonist active by default?
- Is the long-serial engine bigger than one secret or one romance misunderstanding?
- Are support characters designed as agents?
- Does the relationship model fit the channel and genre?
- Is there a clear reason this project is not just a copy of its benchmarks?
- If browsing is unavailable, have assumptions been labeled as baseline industry inference rather than current market fact?
